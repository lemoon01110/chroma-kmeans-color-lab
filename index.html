RGB vs LAB — two ways to measure “close”

Ruler: RGB
Your screen only speaks RGB. Bright red might be (255, 0, 0). A pale sky might be (180, 210, 240). K-Means needs a way to decide which mixes are neighbors — that’s your ruler.

RGB screen recipe

Three numbers the monitor already uses: Red, Green, Blue. Distance = how different those three dials are.

  • Easy to explain: it’s the real storage format.
  • Fast for the computer.
  • Problem: equal jumps in the numbers don’t always look equal to your eyes. A “small” RGB change can look huge (or tiny) depending on the color.

LAB eye-friendly remix

We convert each RGB pixel into a new trio: L = light↔dark, a ≈ green↔red, b ≈ blue↔yellow. Then we measure distance in that remixed space.

  • Built so “close in LAB” is closer to “looks similar.”
  • Same photo, same K — but teams can form differently than in RGB.
  • After clustering we convert flags back to RGB so the screen can draw them.
Ruler in use: RGB Grouping with the raw Red / Green / Blue dials. “Near” means the three numbers are alike — simple, but not always what your eyes would call alike.

RGB, LAB, and the loop — deeper notes

optional
Start here: a pixel is never “just red.” It is always three amounts of light mixed together — Red, Green, Blue. That RGB cube (picture on the left) is how computers store color. K-Means treats each pixel as a point in whatever space you choose, then finds K centers.

Why RGB exists hardware

Screens make every color by mixing three lights. So RGB is the natural language of displays and image files.

  • Example: pure blue ≈ (0, 0, 255)
  • Example: white ≈ (255, 255, 255)
  • Example: dark gray ≈ (40, 40, 40)
  • K-Means in RGB asks: “Are these three dials similar?”

Why invent LAB? perception

Your eyes don’t treat every RGB jump the same. LAB reshapes the map so steps match “looks different” more evenly.

  • L = Lightness (black → white)
  • a ≈ green ↔ red
  • b ≈ blue ↔ yellow
  • K-Means in LAB asks: “Would these look similar to a person?”

Even in LAB mode, the photo still starts as RGB, and the final flags are turned back into RGB so the screen can draw them. LAB is only the temporary map we use to decide what counts as “close.”

1

Guess. Put K flags down in the chosen space (RGB numbers, or LAB remixes).

2

Assign. For each pixel, find the closest flag with that same ruler.

3

Update. Put each flag at the average of its team (average RGB or average LAB, matching the ruler).

4

Converge. Repeat until flags almost stop. Convert centers to RGB and repaint.

Nobody labeled “sky” or “leaf.” The computer only followed a closeness rule in RGB or LAB. Grown-ups call that unsupervised learning. Change the ruler and you can get a different “discovered” palette from the same photo.

Experiments
Quick tries
Ruler for “near”: RGB or LAB
Pixels always begin as RGB (three light dials). RGB mode groups with those dials directly — simple, but not always eye-true. LAB mode first remaps each pixel to Lightness + color-opponent axes, groups there (usually looks cleaner), then converts flags back to RGB to draw.
Where the first flags land
Random = throw flags anywhere (wild early steps). k-means++ = spread the first flags apart on purpose (usually calmer results).
Try a new Guess
Same photo, same K, but brand-new random flag starts. If the colors change, you’ve seen that there isn’t only one “right” answer.
